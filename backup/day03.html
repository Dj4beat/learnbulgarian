Section 1

Numbers 0 to 10

Learn these first — everything else builds on them.

Bulgarian numbers 0–10 are completely regular — no tricks, no exceptions (except the gendered forms of 1 and 2, which we cover in Section 3). Learn these ten words and you have the foundation for all numbers up to a million.

Section 2

Numbers 11 to 20

A simple suffix does all the work.

Numbers 11–19 are formed by taking the single-digit number and adding the suffix -надесет (-nadeset), which literally means "on ten". So 11 = "one-on-ten", 12 = "two-on-ten", and so on. This is perfectly consistent — no exceptions.

💡 Pattern recognition

Notice that 20 uses два-десет (two-ten) while 12 uses два-надесет (two-on-ten). The prefix changes from на- to nothing for the round tens. This same pattern continues: тридесет (30), четиридесет (40), and so on.

Section 3

The gender trap — 1 and 2

The most common number mistake in Bulgarian. Learn it now.

This surprises every English speaker: the numbers 1 and 2 must match the grammatical gender of the noun they are describing. Bulgarian nouns are masculine, feminine or neuter, and 1 and 2 change their endings accordingly.

GenderExample nounOneTwo
Masculineмъж (mazh) — manедин мъж (edin mazh)два мъже (dva mazhe)
Feminineжена (zhena) — womanедна жена (edna zhena)две жени (dve zheni)
Neuterдете (dete) — childедно дете (edno dete)две деца (dve detsa)
🎤 Gendered 1 and 2

🏋 How to know which gender?

A practical guide: nouns ending in a consonant are usually masculine (мъж, ден, стол). Nouns ending in -а or -я are usually feminine (жена, маса). Nouns ending in -е or -о are usually neuter (дете, кафе). You will study this fully on Day 6.

Section 4

Counting in tens and asking prices

The tens pattern plus two essential shopping phrases.

The tens (30, 40, 50…) follow the same two-part logic as 20. To form numbers like 21, 35 or 48, you say the ten, then add и (and), then the unit: двадесет и едно (twenty and one = 21).
